Grants for Cluster Groups (CFP)

Aim and summary

The Ian Ramsey Centre for Science and Religion (IRC) at Oxford University is pleased to offer three cluster group awards of up to US$15,000 each to catalyse the formation of interdisciplinary discussion groups that will (a) bring diverse groups of scholars from sciences and the humanities together to discuss and debate questions related to Special Divine Action (SDA), and (b) host outside speakers to meet with these discussion groups.

Grants will be awarded by competitive evaluation on the basis of proposals. Letters of intent must be received by the IRC no later than January 16, 2015. Complete proposals are due by March 15, 2015, with final award winners informed by March 30, 2015.

Backgrounds and Topics

Is there special divine action (SDA) in the world, beyond the purported effects of a divine first cause? Beliefs about particular kinds of special divine action (such as incarnation, inspirations, grace, miracles, providence, and resurrection) have had an incalculable impact on civilisation, being some of the most important inspirations for art, ethics, music, literature, philosophy, social institutions, theology, the perception of nature, and what human beings can and should hope for. If there is SDA, however, what is such action like and how would we know? Are there particular ways of thinking about the world that make such actions probable, possible or impossible? How does contemporary research in philosophy, theology, and science bear on these questions? What tools of scholarship can and should be used? From the point of view of knowledge and human flourishing, or from a divine perspective, are there advantages and disadvantages for human persons to take a stance for or against the possibility of some or all modes of SDA?

Applicants are free to select research topics on these or other issues across a broad range of matters regarding SDA, but are encouraged to draw up carefully a list of specific and focused questions that their groups propose to address. The following list of sample questions is intended to be strictly illustrative and is not in any way exhaustive:

  • A prominent classical conception of SDA is an intervention model. What analyses of causation fit best with such a model? What problems need to be addressed on this model?
  • Besides the intervention model, what other models of SDA might be viable? Are they compatible with an intervention model, so that there might be multiple types of SDA, or not? What benefits or drawbacks might there be to working with one of these alternative models?
  • What are the useful categorisations of purported SDA? Do grace, inspiration, miracles, and providence cover the entire field of significant theological interest? What are the most appropriate metaphors? How do these varieties of purported SDA impact on perceptions of philosophical and scientific questions, and vice versa?
  • What are the theological ramifications of adopting a non-interventionist model vis-à-vis those of adopting an interventionist model? Does this choice require rethinking other classical theological categories? Why or why not?
  • Is SDA compatible with a strictly physicalist conception of nature? If so, how is it to be distinguished from instances of natural causation? If not, why not?
  • How do views about the scope and limitations of SDA interact with views about the scope and limitations of human personal action?
  • What resources might a dualist analysis (e.g. predicate, property, substance) of human mind-body interactions afford for the analysis of SDA? Are there problems for a dualist analysis that carry over to certain construals of SDA?
  • Is rareness a prerequisite for the religious significance of special divine action? Why or why not? How would the idea that such instances are rare affect the Humean argument against reported miracles from the observed uniformity of nature?
  • What is the proper analysis of special providences – events that, though describable wholly in natural terms, seem to exhibit a timing that is preternaturally auspicious? Is this a form of SDA, or should it be analysed in some other fashion?
  • What is the proper analysis of prophecy? Is this (as Hume suggests) simply another way for the laws of nature to be violated, or is it something else? Under what conditions would it count as SDA? How would the epistemic questions of its evaluation differ from those for (say) turning water into wine or raising someone from the dead?
  • Inspiration: what is the proper epistemic analysis of purported visions, or dreams, or inspiration? Is there a difference of a first-person (tasting a wine?) or third-person perspective? Can inspiration be second-personal? What are the appropriate metaphors for inspiration? What are the relationships among inspiration, perception and insight?

Applicants are encouraged to be creative in their proposals for addressing their chosen questions, but applications that propose to bring together key insights from diverse disciplines will be looked on favourably. Methods of analyses can also be wide-ranging. As the questions above illustrate, applicants may wish to consider the possibility and meaning of a generic kind SDA within a particular worldview, such as naturalism, determinism, occasionalism or informational perspectives on reality. Alternatively, they might want to explore hypothetical questions, such as, if a particular kind of SDA were to take place, would this SDA be recognizable as such, and what are the further implications? In addition, specific or paradigmatic cases of instances of purported SDA could be considered from two or more different disciplines. Along with analysis, they may also wish to examine issues pertaining to the impact and refinement of the metaphoric understanding of SDA.

Favourable consideration will be given to clear, focused research topics and questions. If you have any doubt about whether an idea for a project would be suitable, please contact the IRC: ignacio.silva@theology.ox.ac.uk.

Eligibility

Any institution of higher education around the world can apply and receive one of the three cluster group awards. Each cluster group will have two leaders (drawn from different academic disciplines) and up to eight additional participants. The leaders will be faculty members; the participants may be faculty or graduate students.

The disciplines from which the groups will be drawn are intended to be wide-ranging, including (without being limited to) biology, cosmology, epistemology, mathematics, neuroscience, philosophy, physics, psychology, religious studies, sociology, and theology.

Monetary amount

Awards will consist of US$5,000 for the individual faculty member and US$10,000 for the host institution (including any overheads if required). Host institutions will be required to set aside at least half of the institutional award for professional development or course enhancement opportunities for the award-winning faculty member.

Award details and timescale

Leaders will receive US$2,000 each for developing the syllabus, making meeting arrangements, and purchasing relevant texts. They will also be given US$7,000 (total, not per leader) to pay for guest speakers (at least two), texts for each participant, and other expenses (room costs, refreshments) associated with holding the meetings. Participants will receive US$500 and seminar texts. The maximum budget is US$15,000 (including any overheads if required). Seminars will be required to meet for a combined total of 13 hours over the course of one semester.

In addition, each leader and participant will have the opportunity to receive US$1,000 (or an equal share of $5,000 if there are more than five recipients across all groups) if, within 12 months of the end of the seminar, they are able to submit either: (a) a paper on the seminar’s topic that has been accepted for publication in a peer-reviewed journal or incorporated into a dissertation. Journal articles and dissertations may be across a wide range of fields including, without being limited to, biology, epistemology, mathematics, neuroscience, philosophy, physics, psychology, religious studies, sociology, and theology; or (b) the syllabus for a course in or involving questions of SDA that follows on from the outcome of the seminars and which will be offered within the subsequent academic year.

Proposal submission instructions

Letters of intent are due by January 16, 2015. Invitations for Complete Proposals will be made by January 30, 2015. Complete proposals will be due by March 15, 2015, with final award decisions issued by March 30, 2015 for meetings to begin from July 1, 2015 onwards.

The letter of intent, of not more than 800 words, should include the key questions to be addressed, the significance of the questions, and a brief statement of the proposed approach.

Complete proposals will be invited on a competitive basis. Complete proposals will include a budget, research questions, intended bibliography and a list of guest speakers, as well as the group’s leaders CVs. Complete proposals should be submitted by March 15, 2015. Awards will be announced by March 30, 2015.

Groups are expected to run for a minimum of 13 hours within one semester anytime from July 2015 to June 2016.

Should you require further information about the application process, please refer to the frequently asked questions below. If your question is not answered, please contact ignacio.silva@theology.ox.ac.uk.

Selection criteria

Applications will be judged by two members of the SDA project team at Oxford, assisted by the SDA project leader at Western Michigan University and two members of the project’s Advisory Board.

Criteria for assessments shall be

  • Clarity and focus of the research questions
  • Significance of the research questions for the SDA project
  • Group potential for longer-term engagement in research
  • Interdisciplinary approach
  • Integration of the proposed cluster group within longer-term plans for the applicants and their home institution

Applicants should explain in the project description how their proposed projects meet these criteria.

Successful applicants’ obligations

Successful individuals or research teams will be required to:

  • Carry out the work and activities described in the proposal
  • Prepare a brief group description for the IRC project website
  • Submit a final report.

Application process deadlines

  • January 16, 2015: for the IRC to receive the letter of intent
  • January 30, 2015: for shortlisted applicants to be informed
  • March 15, 2015: for the IRC to receive full proposals
  • March 30, 2015: for award winners to be informed

Projects supported by the grants can be carried out at any time, for up to one semester, between 1 July 2015 and 30 June 2016.

Frequently asked questions

Who can apply?
Any institution of higher education around the world can apply and receive one of the cluster group awards.
Can the cluster group award last longer than a semester?
Cluster groups Awards are intended to offer the opportunity for a group of scholars to meet around themes of special divine action lasting up to one semester. It is hoped, however, that the groups will continue to meet after the award has ceased.
Is there a minimum duration for the cluster group?
The group should meet for a minimum of 13 hours during one semester.
Can the budget for the cluster group be less than US$15,000?
Yes, although we encourage groups to benefit from the full amount available.
When is the deadline?
The deadline for submissions of letters of intent is January 16, 2015.
Which topics are acceptable?
Cluster groups can focus on topics across a broad range of possibilities surrounding issues of special divine action, and the IRC encourages creative applications. Favourable consideration will be given to clear, focused research topics and questions.
Can I submit a cluster group proposal if I have received a course grant from the IRC?
Yes. There are no restrictions arising from participation in other activities of the IRC project.
When should the cluster group begin?
Cluster groups can begin and finish any time between July 2015 and June 2016. We suggest you make early arrangements to allow sufficient time for money allocation and organisation of activities.
How will the grant be disbursed?
It is expected that the leaders will receive directly US$2,000 and that the host institution will receive the rest of the grant around a month before the commencement of the project.
Should my university contribute to the cluster group in any way?
Host universities are not expected to incur in any extra expense to run the cluster groups besides providing work space and facilities for the seminars.
